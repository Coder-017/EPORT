IB MYP Design ePortfolio

This website presents a complete written guide to an IB Middle Years Programme Design ePortfolio, explaining what each criterion requires and how strong evidence can be organized through the full design cycle.

About the Design ePortfolio

An MYP Design ePortfolio is a structured digital record of a studentâ€™s design process. Rather than showing only the final product, it documents the thinking, decisions, testing, and reflections that lead to the product. In the MYP curriculum, this matters because design is assessed as a process of inquiry and iteration, not just an outcome.

The portfolio should show clear progression through four stages. Inquiry and Analysis (Criterion A) establishes the problem, context, and research base. Developing Ideas (Criterion B) converts research into specifications and justified design options. Creating the Solution (Criterion C) demonstrates planning and technical execution. Evaluating (Criterion D) measures success, explains impact, and identifies realistic improvements.

A strong ePortfolio is coherent, evidence-based, and explicit about why decisions were made at each stage. It should read like a design argument supported by data, not a collection of disconnected artifacts.

Criterion A: Inquiry and Analysis

Criterion A focuses on understanding the problem before generating solutions. Students should begin with a clear explanation of the design situation: who the user is, what need exists, and why that need matters. The problem statement must be specific enough to guide design choices later.

What to Include

  • A precise design problem statement connected to a real context
  • Description of the target user and relevant constraints
  • Primary research, such as interviews, surveys, and observations
  • Secondary research, such as articles, case studies, and technical references
  • Analysis of existing products, identifying strengths, weaknesses, and gaps
  • A reasoned justification for choosing this problem as the project focus

Quality Expectations

High-level responses do not just list research findings. They interpret evidence and explain how it influences the direction of the project. Students should show source reliability, compare conflicting findings, and make clear decisions based on insight rather than assumptions.

Criterion B: Developing Ideas

Criterion B is about transforming research into actionable design thinking. Students define measurable design specifications and generate multiple concepts before selecting a final direction.

What to Include

  • A detailed design specification derived directly from Criterion A findings
  • Multiple concept ideas showing variety in form, function, or mechanism
  • Annotated design communication that explains key features and decisions
  • Justification for the selected concept using criteria-based comparison

Design Specification Guidance

Specifications should be testable. For example, instead of writing "easy to use," students should define what usability means in measurable terms, such as setup time, number of steps, or user error rate. Strong specifications include functional, ergonomic, aesthetic, environmental, and safety requirements.

Quality Expectations

The best Criterion B work shows clear iteration. Students should demonstrate that ideas evolved through critique and comparison rather than selecting the first concept generated.

Criterion C: Creating the Solution

Criterion C evaluates how effectively students turn plans into a real solution. The emphasis is on process control, technical competence, and faithful implementation of the intended design.

What to Include

  • A logical production plan with sequencing, timing, and checkpoints
  • Justification of materials, tools, and techniques
  • Evidence of safe working practices and quality control decisions
  • Clear process documentation of key stages and adjustments

Production Process Guidance

A strong process record explains not only what was made, but why changes were introduced. If a step failed or produced an unexpected result, students should document the issue, adaptation, and impact on final quality. This demonstrates authentic technical decision-making.

Quality Expectations

Top responses connect final output quality back to planning discipline. Evidence should show that decisions in materials, assembly, and sequencing were intentional and aligned to specifications.

Criterion D: Evaluating

Criterion D asks students to judge the effectiveness of their final product using evidence. Evaluation must be grounded in the original design specification and supported with test data and user feedback.

Students should evaluate each specification point individually and state whether the final product meets, partially meets, or fails to meet that requirement, using concrete evidence.

Suggested improvements must be realistic and prioritized. Strong responses explain the expected effect of each change on performance, user experience, and feasibility.

Students should discuss user impact and broader implications, including accessibility, environmental footprint, durability, and long-term use context.

Research and Inspiration

This section captures the evidence base that informs design decisions. Research should include user voice, contextual insight, and critical analysis of existing solutions.

Recommended Structure

  • Summary of key user needs discovered through primary research
  • Comparison of existing products and identification of unaddressed gaps
  • Synthesis of secondary research into practical design implications
  • Short rationale showing how findings shaped specification priorities

Inspiration in a text-focused portfolio can be expressed through concept statements, design principles, material strategy notes, and references to successful product features that inform the final direction.

Prototypes and Testing

Prototyping demonstrates iterative refinement. Each prototype version should have a clear objective, corresponding test method, and a documented result that drives the next change.

What to Explain for Each Prototype

  • Purpose of the prototype and what assumption it tested
  • Testing conditions and success criteria
  • Observed performance and user responses
  • Specific improvements made before the next iteration

Effective testing includes both technical checks (stability, durability, safety, precision) and usability checks (comfort, clarity, ease of interaction). Reporting should connect directly to specification points.

Reflections

Reflection should show growth across the project, not just a final opinion. It should identify what changed in your design thinking, how challenges were managed, and which skills improved through practice.

Useful Reflection Prompts

  • How did research change my initial assumptions?
  • What design decision had the biggest effect on outcome quality?
  • Which challenge required the most adaptation and why?
  • What would I do differently if I repeated this project?

Strong reflections reference specific project evidence. Instead of general statements like "I improved time management," explain what planning strategy was adopted and how it changed progress or quality.
